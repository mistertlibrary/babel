Gallery I

The Familiar Territories

The engines most people think of when they think of search. What looks like a neutral utility is, on closer inspection, a set of deeply consequential choices: about what to index, what to surface, who to serve, and what to charge for the privilege. Google's stated mission:"to organize the world's information and make it universally accessible and useful":is either the most ambitious public-interest project in the history of technology or an extraordinarily successful rationalization of monopoly power, depending on how charitable you are feeling. The regional engines here serve as a useful corrective to the assumption that the search landscape is universal. It is not. Where you are shapes what you find.

Gallery II

The Unmapped Web

Before search engines learned to be profitable, the web was navigated by curiosity and accident. These engines are, in aggregate, a counterargument to the premise that the commercial index is the only possible index. Some are built by single developers on home servers. Some are open-source collaborations distributed across volunteer crawlers. Some are principled experiments in what search might look like if it answered to its users rather than its advertisers. Wiby, which indexes only personal and hobbyist pages, puts it plainly: the button that drops you into a random page with no query required is called "Surprise Me." That is a design philosophy, not just a feature. These engines collectively insist that the pre-SEO web:text-rich, human-made, deliberately eccentric:is worth finding.

Gallery III

Archives & Institutional Repositories

The library, like the archive, operates on a different theory of value than the search engine. The search engine asks: what do people want to find? The library asks: what should people be able to find, regardless of whether they know they need it? These are not the same question, and the institutions gathered here have built their search infrastructure around the second one. Some are millennia of human record-keeping made searchable in an afternoon. Some are acts of institutional faith:the Smithsonian's CC0 release of 4.7 million images, the Rijksmuseum's Rijksstudio, the Internet Archive's conviction that everything worth knowing should remain available to everyone, forever:that run directly counter to the dominant logic of digital scarcity. Brewster Kahle, who founded the Internet Archive, called it "universal access to all knowledge." The scale of that ambition is worth sitting with for a moment before you begin searching.
